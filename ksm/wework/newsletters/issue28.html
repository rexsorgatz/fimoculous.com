ISSUE 28 • September 1, 2020
The impact of working from home on collaboration

By now, many of us who’ve worked from home since the beginning of the pandemic lockdown are used to doing a lot over Zoom: being onboarded to a new role, having happy hours, and doing big group projects. But what’s lost in this world of virtual work? Which kind of employee struggles the most while working from their kitchen table? And how does that impact a business in the long term?

A new study done by WeWork in partnership with brightspot strategy, a research and engagement firm, delved into these questions. It surveyed professional office workers across four countries in July 2020, collecting more than 600 responses. They were asked about their personal working style as well as their ability to collaborate while working from home versus while working in the office. What we found were struggles across the board:

  • The ability to meet and brainstorm has dropped an average of 11 percent for all respondents since they began working from home.
  • The ability to maintain social relationships has declined an average of 17 percent.
  • The ability to have unplanned interactions has declined by 25 percent on average.
  • The vast majority (90 percent) of people want to return to the office at least one day a week. Twenty percent of that group want to return five days a week.

See more of the study’s findings—including which kinds of employees are struggling the most—and read the full report at Ideas by WeWork.
